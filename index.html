Practice Trajectory

Three and a Half Steps to This Project

This project doesn't emerge from nowhere. It is the latest iteration in a continuous trajectory of making — each phase depositing something the next one draws on. The arc runs from a CPS archival experiment that first proved the web-to-Unity link, through the installation that grew out of it, through a detour that stress-tested the infrastructure at scale, and finally into the two-part series that pulls everything back together.

How to use this section

Write each phase as a practitioner would — what were you making, what were you learning, and how did it feed forward? This section is evidence for both Concept (the idea didn't arrive fully formed) and Field (a sustained, developing practice). Be specific about what each phase contributed technically and conceptually to where the work sits now.

Concept

Core Idea & Its Evolution

The highest-weighted criterion alongside Cohesion. Markers want to see an original idea that has demonstrably evolved — not stated once and left. Write this so the journey of thought is visible and traceable.

Writing guide
  • State the core concept clearly — what is the central question or provocation?
  • Show why it's original: what gap does it occupy in practice or discourse?
  • Document refinement — how has the concept shifted since the pitch, and why?
  • Connect concept to method: how does the form of the work enact its content?
📝 Core concept statement

Articulate the central idea in 2–4 sentences. What is the work doing, intellectually and experientially? Avoid describing features — describe the argument.

📝 The provocation — why does this need to exist?

What problem, gap, or question in culture, technology, or design is this responding to? This is where your critical framing lives.

📝 How has the concept evolved since the pitch?

Trace the development through the production period. What feedback, research discovery, or making-moment caused you to shift, deepen, or redirect? Name specific turning points.

On conceptual uncertainty

What part of the concept are you still unsure about? What's the open question you're building toward — not the answer you've already landed on?

Field

Situating the Work

Your work doesn't exist in a vacuum. This section makes visible your understanding of the broader landscape — practitioners, theorists, movements, and conversations your project is entering into.

Writing guide
  • Identify 3–5 key works/practitioners — explain how they connect, not just that they do.
  • Show what's already been done and where your work diverges, extends, or challenges it.
  • Articulate significance: why does this inquiry matter to the field right now?
  • Use specific language: "my work extends [X]'s notion of [Y] by…" not vague influence claims.
📝 Position statement — where does this work sit?

In 1–3 paragraphs, locate your project within its disciplinary and critical context. Name the field(s) you're working in or across, and state clearly which conversation you're joining.

📝 Significance — why does this inquiry matter to the field?

Make an argument for why your project's contribution is meaningful or timely. What would be missing from the field's conversation without it?

On field position

Is there a work in the field that makes you slightly anxious because it seems close to yours? What's the crucial difference? That tension often holds the most interesting positioning.

Prototype & Vertical Slice

For interactive projects, Folio 1 requires a usable prototype capturing interaction, look, feel, and capability — without needing additional explanation. This section documents that prototype and what it proves.

Writing guide

05
Technique

Exploiting the Medium

This criterion asks how well you've exploited the specific attributes and aesthetics of your medium — and whether those attributes contribute to an engaging experience. Not what tools you used; why this medium for this idea.

Writing guide
  • Name your medium/stack and argue why it's the right choice for the conceptual goals.
  • Show evidence of technical craft — code snippets, shader work, system diagrams.
  • Explain how the aesthetics of the medium serve the experience, not just its functionality.
  • Document a technical challenge and how you resolved it — iterative technical thinking.
📝 Medium justification — why this stack for this idea?

Make the case for your technical choices. Why Unity and not p5.js? Why a web app front-end? The answer should connect to your conceptual goals — not just what you're familiar with.

📝 How do the medium's aesthetics serve the experience?

Go beyond "it works." Explain how the specific visual, sonic, or interactive qualities contribute to the meaning or affect of the work. What can this medium do that no other could?

📝 A technical challenge you resolved

Document a specific technical problem and how you worked through it. Show the process, not just the result — this is where technical thinking becomes visible.

On the medium's limits

Where does the medium resist you? What can't it do that you wish it could? Knowing the limits of your medium is part of exploiting it well.

06
Cohesion

Internal Consistency & Resolution

Cohesion asks whether all elements are pulling in the same direction — concept, aesthetics, interaction, and sound forming a unified whole with a clear internal logic. This section makes that unity explicit.

Writing guide
  • Demonstrate that visual language, sound, interaction, and concept are in dialogue.
  • Articulate your design principles — the rules that govern every decision in the project.
  • Show a resolved section and explain what makes it resolved.
  • Document a moment where something felt incoherent and how you corrected it.
📝 Design principles — what rules govern every decision?

List 3–5 guiding principles applicable to any new decision in the project. These should be derivable from the concept — structural commitments, not stylistic preferences.

📝 Where did cohesion break down and how did you fix it?

Document a moment when something felt out of place. What was the mismatch, and what did resolving it teach you about the work's internal logic?

📝 What does "resolved" look like in this project?

Define what completion means for a section of this work. What are the conditions that need to be met for something to feel finished? This definition is itself evidence of cohesion.

On internal consistency

Is there a part of the project that still feels like a guest from another work — something that hasn't fully assimilated into the whole? What would it take to resolve that?

Scope, Planning & Iteration

Management marks go to students who demonstrate they can plan, adapt, and document their process rigorously. The marker wants evidence that you've managed this project — not just made things inside it.

Writing guide
  • Show your production plan and note what changed and why.
  • Document scope decisions: what was cut, deferred, or simplified?
  • Show iteration: things made, tested, found lacking, and remade. Name the cycles explicitly.
  • Reflect honestly on what risks remain and what production priorities are for Phase 2.
